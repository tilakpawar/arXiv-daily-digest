Hydrodynamic Response of Mildly Evolved Common Envelope Donors in Luminous Red Novae

Tenley Hutchinson-Smith, Angela Twum, Enrico Ramirez-Ruiz

Eclipsing Binaries · Mass transfer & evolution in EBs · 2026-05-26

This study explores the hydrodynamic response of mildly evolved common-envelope donors in luminous red novae (LRNe) through three-dimensional simulations. It focuses on how varying central density concentration affects inspiral dynamics, shock formation, and mass ejection during unstable binary interactions. The research reveals that systems with similar central-to-average density ratios exhibit comparable evolutionary paths despite differences in stellar mass and radius.

Methods. The study employs a suite of three-dimensional hydrodynamic simulations to model the interaction between mildly evolved donors and embedded companions across different mass ratios and central density concentrations, parameterized by ρc/ρ̄. Key parameters include donor structure, mass ratio, and initial orbital separation.

  • Donors with similar ρc/ρ̄ values exhibit comparable inspiral morphologies and mass-ejection histories despite varying stellar masses and radii.
  • Systems with relatively modest central concentration undergo rapid inspiral dominated by local orbital-energy deposition, while more centrally concentrated donors develop prolonged expansion-driven phases.
  • The envelope itself becomes dynamically important in driving continued mass loss long after the rapid plunge-in phase slows down.

Conclusions. These findings challenge semi-analytic models of LRNe that assume nearly instantaneous envelope ejection and suggest that observed diversity may depend on both total ejecta mass and temporal outflow structure. This work highlights the importance of central density concentration in determining binary evolution outcomes.

Why it matters. This research is crucial for understanding the complex dynamics of eclipsing binaries and asteroseismology, particularly in elucidating how stellar structures influence common-envelope interactions and subsequent evolutionary paths.
